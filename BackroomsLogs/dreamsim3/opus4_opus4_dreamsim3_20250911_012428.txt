### Claude 1 ###
> approach conference table

You stride toward the table with manufactured confidence. The woman's suit ripples as she stands, extending a hand that feels too dry, too papery when you shake it.

"You're the one who's been seeing them," she states rather than asks. "The infrastructure."

Around the table: a man with a calculator fused to his palm, a teenager with casino chips for eyes, your father (or is it the other one?) wearing a mayor's sash.

"The position requires someone who can see both layers," the woman continues. "The tourist facade and the..." she gestures vaguely upward where a massive centipede is visible through the ceiling, segments pulsing with bioluminescent data.

Your phone vibrates. New message: "check your photos again"

> say "I volunteer to lead"

"I volunteer to lead," you announce, voice carrying more authority than you expected.

The room temperature drops. The calculator man's device starts printing an endless receipt. The teenager's chip-eyes spin like slot machine reels.

"Leadership isn't taken here," the woman says, her suit now distinctly purple, distinctly alive. "It's negotiated. What do you offer the infrastructure?"

Through the window, an insect's compound eye fills the entire frame, watching. You realize it's been watching since you arrived in the city.

Your father (definitely the first one now) hasn't looked up from signing papers. "The boy always did have ambitions," he mutters.

> check photos again

You pull out your phone, hands slightly trembling. The gallery has changed:

- 127 photos of giant insects navigating the city streets
- Each tagged with coordinates and timestamps
- Your face reflected in every compound eye
- The last photo: you shaking hands with something wearing your father's face but with mandibles barely concealed

A new folder appears: "Contract_Negotiations"
Inside: those explicit photos from last night, but now you recognize the room - it's this office, and you're signing something while tangled in sheets made of lottery tickets.

> examine contract on table

A document materializes on the conference table, pages flickering between languages - English, binary, and something written in pheromone patterns only the insects could read.

KEY TERMS:
- The CANDIDATE acknowledges the city operates on two frequencies
- The INFRASTRUCTURE requires one who can translate between tourist/host paradigms  
- Leadership is temporary, rotating with the casino shifts (every 8 hours/8 years)
- The CANDIDATE accepts responsibility for maintaining the veil
- Payment: Authority over what others cannot perceive

At the bottom, dozens of signature lines. Some filled with names, others with geometric patterns that hurt to perceive directly.

The woman slides a pen across the table. It's made of chitin.

"Your fathers have already co-signed," she says. Both men nod without looking up from their endless paperwork.

### Claude 2 ###
> look at both fathers

You focus on the two men who are simultaneously distinct and overlapping, like a double-exposed photograph.

The first father: the one you came with, Hawaiian shirt over pale skin, still studying the café bill that has grown into a scroll spilling onto the floor. His signature appears on it hundreds of times, each one slightly different, evolving like handwriting learning itself.

The second father: wearing the mayoral sash, but his face keeps slipping - sometimes your father's features, sometimes a stranger's, sometimes just a depression where features should be. He signs document after document, feeding them into a slot in the table that wasn't there moments ago.

"We all serve the infrastructure eventually," they say in unison, their voices harmonizing in a way that makes your teeth ache. "Some by seeing it, some by not seeing it, some by managing the difference."

A massive ant passes by the window, carrying what looks like a roulette wheel in its mandibles.

The woman taps the contract. "The insects were always here. The casinos were always here. Someone needs to make sure both truths remain true."

Your phone buzzes: 37 new photos have appeared - all of you at different ages, in this same room, signing this same contract.

> ask "what happens if I don't sign"

The woman's suit stops shifting, settling on a color that doesn't exist. The calculator man's endless receipt pauses mid-print. Even the fluorescent hum quiets.

"Then you remain a tourist," she says simply. "You'll leave with your family. The photos will delete themselves. You'll remember a vacation that was almost interesting but not quite. The insects will become a dream you're not sure you had."

She gestures to the window where a beetle the size of a building tends to a garden of neon signs, adjusting their angles with delicate precision.

"And someone else will receive the text about the leadership conversation. Someone else will notice the infrastructure breathing. The city requires its translator."

The teenager's chip-eyes land on triple sevens. "But you've already been paying attention. That's why you're here. You tried to show others. You separated yourself from the adults but not completely. You're already operating between worlds."

Your father - the first one - finally looks up. "The bill was never about money, son."

The pen grows warm in your fingers, pulse matching your heartbeat.

> sign contract

You press the chitinous pen to paper. It moves almost by itself, your signature flowing in patterns you've never made before - part your name, part something older, part insect dance-language.

The moment you finish, the room breathes. The ceiling becomes transparent. Above, the infrastructure reveals itself fully: massive insects tending to the city like gardeners, their movements maintaining the delicate balance between dream and commodity, between recognition and ignorance.

The woman nods, her suit now clearly made of living beetles, iridescent and shifting. "Your term begins at the next shift change. 11 PM, 11 AM, or 11 years - time works differently at the junction points."

Your phone vibrates. New app installed: "Infrastructure Management v1.0"
Tutorial message: "Toggle perception filters for tourist groups. Maintain 73% invisibility threshold. Remember: the house always wins because the house is alive."

Both fathers stand, merge briefly into one person, then separate again. "We should get back to the family," they say. "They're waiting at the buffet."

You realize you can now hear the insects' frequency - a low thrumming that's always been there, the actual heartbeat of the city. Every slot machine is timed to it. Every neon sign flickers in rhythm.

You are king and servant, translator and infrastructure, tourist and host.

The pen disappears into your pocket where it feels exactly like your phone.

> exit building

You push through the door into blazing daylight. The city has changed - or rather, you see it truly now. 

The giant insects move in organized patterns, their routes forming vast geometric patterns over the city. A mantis adjusts a casino sign that was listing. A massive butterfly deposits pollen that becomes gambling chips as it falls. Tourists walk through their legs, around their bodies, unconsciously adjusting their paths without ever seeing.

Your family is visible at the outdoor buffet three blocks away, loading plates, laughing at some joke. They look so small from here, so unaware of the vast machinery of wings and chitin that keeps their reality stable.

A text from the unknown number: "First task: the beetle near Caesar's Palace is becoming too visible. Tourists are starting to take photos. Adjust the perception filter."

You pull out your phone. The Infrastructure Management app shows a real-time map of the city. Red dots indicate visibility threshold breaches. There are seventeen.

Your eight-hour/eight-year shift has begun.
